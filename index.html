Introduction

As an elite institution nestled in the heart of the American South, Duke University has an admittedly complicated and messy relationship with racial equality and social justice. Although Duke is presently working towards becoming a more inclusive and equitable environment, remnants of our racist and discriminatory history continue to affect the power play that occurs today between students, administration, and staff members at this university. We started off the project with the aim of lifting up narratives from marginalized groups that have traditionally been silenced or unheard—mainly the staff and people of color that work at the university. However, we realized that it would be more beneficial to deconstruct themes of race and privilege by also including perspectives from various stakeholders of the university, such as administration, student activists, as well as workers. By looking at three different categories of stakeholders, we can examine how the varying levels of power in this university interact—who really gets a say in how important decisions get made on campus? Are all members of our university well represented and respected? How does important change, like improvements to social and economic justice, get implemented? In order to answer these questions and more, we conducted interviews with three people from the following three categories: The Forgotten, The Change Agents, and The Top Dogs.

Introduction

As an elite institution nestled in the heart of the American South, Duke University has an admittedly complicated and messy relationship with racial equality and social justice. Although Duke is presently working towards becoming a more inclusive and equitable environment, remnants of our racist and discriminatory history continue to affect the power play that occurs today between students, administration, and staff members at this university. We started off the project with the aim of lifting up narratives from marginalized groups that have traditionally been silenced or unheard—mainly the staff and people of color that work at the university. However, we realized that it would be more beneficial to deconstruct themes of race and privilege by also including perspectives from various stakeholders of the university, such as administration, student activists, as well as workers. By looking at three different categories of stakeholders, we can examine how the varying levels of power in this university interact—who really gets a say in how important decisions get made on campus? Are all members of our university well represented and respected? How does important change, like improvements to social and economic justice, get implemented? In order to answer these questions and more, we conducted interviews with three people from the following three categories: The Forgotten, The Change Agents, and The Top Dogs.

"The Forgotten"

Often overlooked by the students and administrators of our institution are the people that keep it running. The university’s service employees—consisting largely of people of color from the Durham community—clean up our facilities, prepare the food that we eat, and help maintain the preserve the institution. They are, however, often minimized, neglected, and consequently forgotten by those who benefit most from their work. As our university reckons with the confluence of racism and privilege that had stained its past, it’s necessary to examine how it lingers on today towards the treatment of these workers. In the effort to lift their hidden stories, struggles and perspectives, we interviewed three employees from different sectors of the university: Tamika Robinson (cook at Marketplace), Jessie Harris (housekeeper in Giles House), and Oscar Dantzler (custodian of Duke Chapel).

"The Change Agents"

Student leaders play a crucial role in catalyzing change at this university. In recent years, student leaders protested the renegotiation of worker’s hours, pushed for higher salaries, and advocated for hiring reform. How do students use their voice to create a lasting impact that betters our institution as a whole? To get a better understanding of the stories, motivations, and perspectives of student leaders and activists, we interviewed three prominent leaders that all play a different role on campus: Kennedy Ware (president of the Black Student Alliance, BSA), Uwa Ihionkhan (representative in Duke Student Government, DSG), and Trey Walk (leader of a social activism group, People’s State of the University, PSOTU).

"The Top Dogs"

Most of the day-to-day policies that affect large swathes of the Duke community are implemented and executed by university administrators—the top dogs. As of late, they have received both criticism and acclaim, some arguing that they are too slow, too opaque, and too unilateral in responding to racial justice issues on campus, and others arguing the opposite. The actions that have emanated from the oak walls of the Allen Building, moreover, have fed into this conflict of opinion; the bargaining of workers ours was widely criticized for being pursued unilaterally without workers voices, yet its actions of “banning the box” has been widely applauded for increasing job accessibility. Now, in the effort to clear the noise perpetuated by misunderstanding and palace intrigue, we’ve interviewed three university administrators whose actions’ most influence workers lives: Sally Kornbluth (Provost), Ben Reese (Vice President for Institutional Equity) and Joe Gonzales (Dean of Housing and Residence Life).
